# Call Scoring Rubric Guide

This document defines how Argos should score sales calls. You do not need to be technical to use it. Your job is to define what good looks like, what weak execution looks like, and how managers should score calls consistently based on what is actually said and done on the call.

---

## How This Works (Non-Technical Summary)

When a rep uploads a call recording, Argos will:

1. Transcribe the call into text
2. Send that transcript to an AI along with this rubric
3. Score the call against the standards below
4. Store and display those scores to managers and reps

**The AI is only as good as the rubric.** If the rubric is vague, inflated, or inconsistent, the scores will be unreliable. If the rubric is specific, transcript-observable, and grounded in real sales behavior, the scores will be useful for coaching and performance management.

---

## Manager Calibration

### 1. Identify the call type before scoring

Not every call should contain a full pitch, objections, or a late-stage close. Label the call first:

- **Early-stage discovery:** heavy on frame and discovery; pitch may be light; objections may be minimal; close is usually a next-step commitment.
- **Demo / solution exploration:** discovery, transition, and pitch are in scope; objections may appear; close should secure the next step.
- **Technical validation:** the "pitch" is often requirements-fit and implementation clarity; objections may be technical; close is alignment on what must happen next.
- **Late-stage commercial / procurement:** discovery is narrower; objections and closing carry more weight.
- **Renewal / expansion:** discovery focuses on value realized, new needs, and buying path; close is commitment to the expansion or renewal plan.

### 2. Use `N/A` only when a category is truly out of scope

If a category is genuinely not applicable because of the call type, mark it `N/A` and redistribute that weight proportionally across the remaining categories.

Rules:

1. Do **not** mark a category `N/A` because the rep skipped it.
2. Do mark a category `N/A` when the purpose of the call made it inappropriate.
3. Closing is rarely `N/A`. Even technical or early-stage calls should end with a concrete next step.

### 3. Keep the evidence standard practical

- For **low-weight categories** like `Build Rapport` and `Transition to Pitch`, one strong example is enough.
- For **higher-weight categories**, look for `2–3` concrete moments.
- Score what is observable: questions asked, summaries given, objections clarified, commitments secured, next steps booked.

### 4. Keep the categories clean

- **Build Rapport:** tone, professionalism, and the quality of the opening connection
- **Set a Strong Frame:** the opening contract for how this call will run
- **In-Depth Discovery:** diagnosis and qualification
- **Transition to Pitch:** the bridge from verified pain to relevant solution discussion
- **Pitch/Demo:** how well the solution is mapped to stated needs
- **Overcome Objections:** how pushback is clarified, handled, and resolved
- **Closing:** whether the rep secures a specific commitment, owner, and next step

Avoid awarding the same behavior twice. For example, if stakeholder identification is uncovered in discovery, do not automatically count it again in closing unless the rep turns it into a concrete next-step plan.

## The 7 Categories

### 1. Build Rapport

**Weight:** `5`

**What it measures:**  
How effectively the rep creates a professional, comfortable tone early in the call without wasting time or sounding forced.

**What good looks like:**

- Opens with a brief, relevant human connection and moves into the business purpose quickly
- Shows respect for time
- Sounds composed, professional, and easy to engage with

**What poor performance looks like:**

- Long, generic small talk
- Forced friendliness or tone mismatch
- Abrupt pitch-first opening with no connection

**Scoring rubric:**

| Score Range | What it looks like |
|---|---|
| 80-100 | The rep establishes a professional tone quickly, makes a relevant connection, and pivots smoothly into the purpose of the meeting with minimal filler. |
| 50-79 | The rep is polite but generic. Rapport does not hurt the call, but it does not meaningfully improve trust or comfort either. |
| 0-49 | The opening is awkward, rambling, overly casual, or purely transactional. The rep either wastes time or jumps straight into a pitch. |

**Phrases and patterns to look for:**

- Good:
  - "I saw you're focused on [initiative]. Is that the main reason you took this meeting?"
  - "I want to be mindful of time. Do you still have [X] minutes?"
  - "If I make a wrong assumption, stop me and I'll adjust."
- Bad:
  - "How are things?" followed by several minutes of unrelated talk
  - "Let me tell you about our company..." as the opener
  - Repetitive filler with no substance: "Awesome, awesome... love it..."

**Coaching guidance:**

- Train the first 60 seconds: one relevant hook, one time check, then purpose.
- Replace generic small talk with a trigger question tied to why the prospect took the meeting.
- Coach reps toward "pleasant and direct," not "chatty."

---

### 2. Set a Strong Frame

**Weight:** `15`

**What it measures:**  
Whether the rep establishes a clear contract for the call: time, purpose, agenda, and how the conversation will run.

**What good looks like:**

- Confirms time and sets an agenda with buyer agreement
- Explains the purpose of the call clearly
- Sets expectations for how the call will end
- Redirects drift without sounding rigid or defensive

**What poor performance looks like:**

- No agenda or structure
- Immediate detours or random topic switching
- Demoing too early when discovery should happen first

**Scoring rubric:**

| Score Range | What it looks like |
|---|---|
| 80-100 | Time, purpose, and agenda are clear and accepted. The rep leads the call, uses clean transitions, and does not let the conversation drift without resetting it. |
| 50-79 | The rep attempts a frame but does not secure agreement or maintain it consistently. The call drifts and the rep becomes reactive. |
| 0-49 | There is no real frame. The call turns into an ad hoc Q&A, a generic overview, or a buyer-led wander. |

**Phrases and patterns to look for:**

- Good:
  - "Here's what I suggest: I'll ask a few questions, then we'll decide what's worth looking at today. Sound reasonable?"
  - "At the end, I'll recap what I heard and we'll agree on next steps."
  - "Let's park that for a moment and come back after I understand [X]."
- Bad:
  - "So what do you want to see?"
  - "We can cover anything you want."
  - "I can jump into a demo now."

**Coaching guidance:**

- Standardize a frame script: time, purpose, agenda, and end-of-call plan.
- Teach redirect language so reps can regain control without sounding combative.
- Coach reps to earn the right to demo rather than defaulting to it.

---

### 3. In-Depth Discovery

**Weight:** `15`

**What it measures:**  
The rep's ability to diagnose and qualify with commercial judgment: pain, impact, urgency, consequence of delay, alternatives, stakeholders, and decision criteria.

**What good looks like:**

- Moves from symptoms to stakes, not just facts
- Surfaces why now, what happens if nothing changes, and what the status quo is costing
- Identifies alternatives, internal priorities, and decision criteria
- Distinguishes a real project from general curiosity

**What poor performance looks like:**

- Surface-level questions with no impact or urgency
- No consequence of delay
- No understanding of alternatives or buying path
- Accepts vague answers and moves on

**Scoring rubric:**

| Score Range | What it looks like |
|---|---|
| 80-100 | The rep uncovers a buyer-confirmed problem, clear impact, why now, consequence of delay, and enough qualification context to guide the next step. The call makes it clear this is either a real initiative or not. |
| 50-79 | Discovery is partly effective, but at least one major gap remains: urgency is unclear, impact is weak, alternatives are missing, or decision criteria are vague. |
| 0-49 | Discovery is shallow, scattered, or cut short by premature pitching. The rep leaves the business case and buying path unclear. |

**Phrases and patterns to look for:**

- Good:
  - "What's driving this now versus six months from now?"
  - "If you don't solve this, what gets worse?"
  - "What have you tried already?"
  - "How will you evaluate options?"
  - "Who else will weigh in on this?"
  - "Are you comparing this to anything else or keeping the status quo?"
- Bad:
  - "So how many users do you have?" with no tie to impact
  - "We can definitely help." before urgency is established
  - Repeated "Got it" with no follow-up
  - Never asking about timeline, alternatives, or decision criteria

**Coaching guidance:**

- Make `why now`, `cost of delay`, and `alternatives` mandatory parts of discovery.
- Train reps to ask follow-ups when answers are vague: "Can you give me an example?" or "What number sits behind that?"
- Coach reps to test seriousness, not just gather information.

---

### 4. Transition to Pitch

**Weight:** `5`

**What it measures:**  
Whether the rep earns the right to present by confirming pain and linking the upcoming solution discussion to what the buyer already said.

**What good looks like:**

- Recaps the buyer's pain and impact in plain language
- Gets buyer agreement before shifting
- States what will be shown and why it matters

**What poor performance looks like:**

- Abrupt pivot into product
- Vague recap that is more about the rep than the buyer
- No clear connection between discovery and the pitch

**Scoring rubric:**

| Score Range | What it looks like |
|---|---|
| 80-100 | The rep summarizes the problem clearly, gets audible agreement, and explains exactly what they will show and why it is relevant. |
| 50-79 | There is some recap or bridge, but it is incomplete, generic, or not confirmed by the buyer. |
| 0-49 | The rep jumps into the solution without confirming pain, impact, or relevance. |

**Phrases and patterns to look for:**

- Good:
  - "Let me recap to make sure I'm tracking: [pain + impact]. Is that accurate?"
  - "Given that, I'll focus on [1-2 items] that address [issue]. Want me to walk you through it?"
- Bad:
  - "Alright, let me show you our platform."
  - "We're a full-suite solution..."
  - "Anyway..."

**Coaching guidance:**

- Train a short bridge: recap, confirm, map, permission.
- Require buyer agreement before shifting into solution mode.
- Coach reps to keep the pitch scope tight and relevant.

---

### 5. Pitch / Demo

**Weight:** `15`

**What it measures:**  
How well the rep presents the solution in a way that is specific to the buyer's verified needs, uses proof, and checks understanding.

**What good looks like:**

- Maps each point to a known buyer problem or priority
- Uses proof: customer example, metric, before/after result, or implementation detail
- Checks for understanding and adjusts if needed
- Avoids overlong feature tours

**What poor performance looks like:**

- Generic product walkthrough
- Big claims with no proof
- No check-ins or ignored confusion
- Demo loses the thread and becomes a feature dump

**Scoring rubric:**

| Score Range | What it looks like |
|---|---|
| 80-100 | The rep clearly maps the solution to stated needs, supports major claims with proof, and gets buyer responses that show relevance and clarity. |
| 50-79 | Some parts are relevant, but other parts drift into generic territory. Proof is limited and check-ins are shallow. |
| 0-49 | The rep delivers a feature dump with weak relevance, weak proof, and poor control of buyer understanding. |

**Phrases and patterns to look for:**

- Good:
  - "You said the bottleneck is [X]. Here's the exact step this removes."
  - "Here's how teams like yours measured success."
  - "Does this match your process?"
  - "Which part matters most to your team?"
- Bad:
  - "Let me show you all the modules."
  - "It's super easy to implement."
  - "Most customers love this feature." with no tie to buyer need
  - Continuing the demo after clear buyer confusion

**Coaching guidance:**

- Teach reps to map before they show.
- Build proof by use case, not by feature list.
- Require one check-in per major section of the pitch.

---

### 6. Overcome Objections

**Weight:** `15`

**What it measures:**  
How effectively the rep handles pushback: clarifies the real concern, isolates it, responds with specifics, and confirms whether the issue is resolved enough to proceed.

**What good looks like:**

- Acknowledges the concern without collapsing or arguing
- Clarifies what the objection really is
- Isolates whether it is the only blocker
- Responds with specifics, proof, or a credible plan
- Confirms whether the response moved the buyer forward

**What poor performance looks like:**

- Defensive rebuttals
- Answering the wrong objection
- Immediate discounting or concessions
- Long monologues with no confirmation

**Scoring rubric:**

| Score Range | What it looks like |
|---|---|
| 80-100 | The rep follows a clear path: acknowledge, clarify, isolate, respond with specifics, and confirm. Buyer language shows movement rather than repeated resistance. |
| 50-79 | The rep addresses the objection, but misses a key step such as isolation, proof, or confirmation. Concerns partially linger. |
| 0-49 | The rep becomes defensive, vague, or evasive. The objection remains unresolved or the rep gives up ground too quickly. |

**Phrases and patterns to look for:**

- Good:
  - "When you say it's expensive, what are you comparing it to?"
  - "What specifically feels risky about implementation?"
  - "If we solved that concern, would you move to the next step?"
  - "Does that address it enough to proceed?"
- Bad:
  - "We're not the cheapest, but we're the best."
  - "That won't be a problem."
  - "We can probably work something out on price." before diagnosis
  - Long rebuttals with no check for whether the buyer is satisfied

**Coaching guidance:**

- Train a 5-step objection routine: acknowledge, clarify, isolate, respond with specifics, confirm.
- Build objection playbooks by objection type.
- Coach tone and pacing, not just answer quality.

---

### 7. Closing

**Weight:** `30`

**What it measures:**  
Whether the rep secures a clear commitment and a concrete next-step plan with owners, timing, and stage-appropriate movement.

**What good looks like:**

- Recaps the agreed problem and desired outcome briefly
- Makes a direct ask for the correct next-stage commitment
- Locks next step, owner, attendee list, and timing
- Forces clarity when the buyer is vague or non-committal

**What poor performance looks like:**

- Vague follow-up language
- No explicit ask
- Buyer-owned next steps with no time bound
- Avoiding pushback by settling for "send me something"

**Scoring rubric:**

| Score Range | What it looks like |
|---|---|
| 80-100 | The rep secures a specific next-step commitment with clear owners and timing. The call ends with an agreed plan and real momentum. |
| 50-79 | The rep discusses next steps, but they remain soft, incomplete, or fragile. The deal advances, but the path is loose. |
| 0-49 | The rep accepts vagueness, leaves scheduling open-ended, or ends without a real commitment. |

**Phrases and patterns to look for:**

- Good:
  - "Based on what we covered, the next step I recommend is [X]. Are you comfortable doing that?"
  - "Who needs to be in the next conversation?"
  - "Let's get it on the calendar."
  - "Here are the next steps and owners..."
  - "If we don't schedule the next step, what's the main reason?"
- Bad:
  - "I'll send a recap and we can reconnect."
  - "Just take a look and get back to me."
  - "Do you have any other questions?" as the only close
  - "I'll follow up next week." with no agreement

**Coaching guidance:**

- Require `next step + date/time + attendees` on every call.
- Coach reps to close to the stage, not always to signature.
- Train reps to diagnose non-commitment instead of accepting soft stalls.

---

## Final Notes

- Score what happened, not what the rep meant to do.
- Do not inflate scores because the rep was energetic or likable.
- Penalize premature pitching and soft closes.
- If two managers would not score the same call similarly using this rubric, the standard is still too vague.

This document is intended to be used directly as the scoring standard for Argos call evaluation.
